# U.S. Economy Snapshot

Generated from current public data on March 26, 2026.

## Executive Take

The U.S. economy appears to be cooling materially, with a labor market that is softening but not breaking. Inflation has come down from its post-pandemic highs, and the latest short-run price readings suggest that inflation momentum has re-accelerated somewhat. At the same time, interest rates remain elevated enough that policy still leans restrictive, but not by a wide margin, which helps explain why growth-sensitive sectors such as housing and manufacturing are not sending an unambiguously strong signal.

- Real GDP was up **2.03% year over year** as of **Q4 2025**, with the latest quarterly annualized pace at **0.65%**.
- The unemployment rate was **4.4%** in **February 2026**, compared with a 12-month low of **4.1%**.
- Nonfarm payrolls increased by an average of **5,667** jobs per month over the latest three months through **February 2026**.
- CPI inflation was **2.43% year over year** in **February 2026**, while the 3-month annualized pace was **2.98%**.
- Core PCE inflation was **3.06% year over year** in **January 2026**, with a 3-month annualized pace of **3.66%**.
- The effective federal funds rate averaged **3.64%** in **February 2026**. Against core PCE inflation, that implies a rough real policy rate of **0.58%**.
- The 10-year minus 2-year Treasury spread was **0.46 percentage points** on **March 26, 2026**.
- Industrial production was **1.44% year over year** in **February 2026**.

- Housing starts were **9.50% year over year** in **January 2026**.

## Nuanced Read

1. **Growth is slower, not necessarily stalled.** Real GDP is still above year-ago levels, but the most recent quarterly annualized pace is much more informative about current momentum than the year-over-year number. That leaves the economy in a cooling phase rather than a clean boom.

2. **The labor market is losing altitude gradually.** Unemployment remains low by historical standards, but it has moved off its cycle floor. Payroll growth is still positive, yet the three-month average of roughly 5,667 jobs per month is a better gauge of trend than any single month, and it points to a slower hiring environment.

3. **Inflation momentum needs to be read on more than one horizon.** The gap between year-over-year inflation and the 3-month annualized pace matters. When short-run measures run above year-over-year inflation, it usually means the last few months have been firmer than the broader 12-month trend, which is a reminder that the path back to target is rarely smooth.

4. **Policy restraint is still visible, but less overwhelming than before.** A fed funds rate near 3.64% with core PCE inflation at 3.06% means real short rates remain positive. Real short rates remain positive, but only modestly so, which suggests monetary policy may still be restraining demand without delivering the kind of heavy drag usually associated with a sharp downturn.

5. **Consumers are not sending a recessionary signal yet.** Real retail sales matter more than nominal sales because they strip out price effects. A positive real spending trend would suggest households are still supporting the expansion; a flat or negative one would warn that consumption is no longer carrying the cycle as effectively.

6. **The rate curve is useful, but it should not be read mechanically.** The 10Y-2Y spread at 0.46 percentage points captures market expectations about future policy and growth. Its signal is more informative when paired with labor data, claims, and credit-sensitive activity rather than treated as a stand-alone recession timer.
